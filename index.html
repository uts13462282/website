Russian Flu

When the Europeans who arrived in Australia, they also brought with them the contagious diseases of Europe. Flu pandemics in Europe were spreading faster than ever before in the nineteenth century, putting Australia at risk of contracting them as well. The first major flu outbreak in Victoria occurred in 1860–61, and it grew more common in the 1880s (McKinnon, 2020).

“The Russian flu is the epidemic that is described as mystery flu because it came in three waves then just like that, it vanished, taking many lives. It is also described as the most similar to the present coronavirus. ”

Russian Flu in the News

The first instances were documented in Australia towards the end of March 1890, and the epidemic raged for three or four months, killing 164 people in the state of Victoria. The epidemic returned the following year, even more severe than it had been in Europe.


The sickness lasted for another decade, according to the Commonwealth Yearbook. Between 1893 and 1904, less than 150 persons died from influenza-related illnesses, with over 1,000 of those deaths occurring during a single year. The Russian flu is the epidemic that is described as mystery flu because it came in three waves then just like that, it vanished, taking many lives. It is also described as the most similar to the present coronavirus.

Spanish Flu

Progress

Australia was also hit by the Spanish flu in late 1918. After hearing about the epidemic's progress in Europe in late summer of 1918 and subsequently in September. Australian officials instituted quarantine procedures on October 18, 1918, at all Australian ports.

Social impacts

A variety of organizations, including schools, churches, theaters, bars, horse races, and agricultural fairs, were had to either cancel their events or postpone them. As a direct consequence of this circumstance, not only were there problems with finances, but there were also significant disruptions to the provision of services related to education, entertainment, travel, and shopping.

Death toll

As many as 2 million Australians (two-fifths of the population) may have been afflicted with influenza in the six months from February 1919, according to estimates. As a result, over 5,000 families were disrupted and over 5,000 children were left without one or both of their parents.

Fight againt the pandemic

Tens of thousands of regular Australians rose to the challenge and helped in the fight against it. During World War II, a number of different types of organizations, including churches, civic leaders, council employees, teachers, nurses, and organizations like the Red Cross, increased their levels of involvement and community service

Reflection from the author

Throughout the nineteenth century, epidemics of smallpox and other diseases plagued Victoria, as did the "Russian flu" (1890), "Spanish flu" (1919), and "Asian flu" (1957) pandemics, among many others. Australians' lives have been forever changed by each episode. They weren't just health-related emergencies (Bennett, 2009). They altered political and social life, intensified existing tensions between the Commonwealth and the states, as well as creating new forms of communal strength. The last 200 years have seen a slew of diseases and pandemics in Australia. These events are compared, and what lessons may be learned from the past is discussed to understand the current problem. However, it also highlights the limitations of extrapolating lessons from the past and the necessity of considering the unique features and challenges of each crisis as it arises. Mortality rates are the most striking difference between current outbreaks and those that have occurred in the past (Campbell, 1983).
